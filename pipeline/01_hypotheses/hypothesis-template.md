# Hypothesis Template

## Title

Write a short, precise, and falsifiable statement of the claim.

The title should identify the main mechanism or expected effect, not just the topic.

## Level of analysis

State the main level at which the hypothesis operates:

- person,
- household,
- group,
- institution,
- state,
- market,
- or cross-level.

## Domain

State the main investment domain or domains in scope.

Examples:

- health,
- education,
- time allocation,
- financial capital,
- trust,
- coordination,
- industrial policy,
- security,
- spiritual practice,
- institutional resilience.

## Core claim

State the hypothesis in one clear falsifiable sentence.

A strong formulation usually has the form:

> When X condition holds, Y outcome should occur through Z mechanism, relative to baseline B.

## Why it matters

Explain why this claim matters for:

- interpretation,
- prioritization,
- allocation,
- strategy,
- or life planning.

## Mechanism

Describe the causal chain in plain language.

Useful prompts:

1. What changes first?
2. Which actors or systems react?
3. Through what channels does the effect propagate?
4. Why should the outcome differ from baseline?

## Required inputs

Reference the relevant material from `00_inputs/`.

Typical dependencies may include:

- actor classes,
- asset or capital classes,
- event classes,
- indicator families,
- assumptions,
- coverage boundaries,
- snapshots,
- constraints,
- priorities,
- person or group types.

## Expected observable signature

Describe what should be visible if the hypothesis is supported.

Possible signatures include:

- relative repricing,
- improved resilience,
- lower downside frequency,
- faster recovery,
- increased optionality,
- lower friction,
- higher trust,
- better coordination,
- higher skill compounding,
- reduced crisis intensity,
- lagged but durable gains.

## Null / baseline comparison

Define the simplest alternative explanation.

Examples:

- no differentiated effect,
- general sentiment shock only,
- random fluctuation,
- broad risk-off response,
- no compounding beyond normal drift,
- gains fully explained by outside variables.

## Falsifiers

List outcomes that would count against the claim.

A good falsifier is concrete, observable, and not impossible by construction.

## Gain dimensions in scope

State what kinds of gains or losses are being measured.

Possible dimensions:

- money,
- time,
- health,
- skill,
- optionality,
- trust,
- resilience,
- legitimacy,
- coordination,
- meaning.

Do not assume all dimensions collapse into a single score unless the run explicitly defines such a score.

## Distribution and externalities

If relevant, explain:

- who gains,
- who loses,
- whether the outcome is positive-sum, zero-sum, or mixed,
- and what collateral damage must be tracked.

## Practical relevance

Explain how the result could matter for downstream use.

Examples:

- portfolio construction,
- risk management,
- life-plan compilation,
- policy prioritization,
- educational design,
- health routines,
- institutional review processes,
- coordination protocols.

## Caveats and boundary conditions

List ambiguity sources, context dependencies, and reasons the claim may fail.

Useful prompts:

- Which assumptions are fragile?
- Which omitted variables could dominate?
- In which life stages, sectors, or regimes would the claim likely fail?
- What would make the claim non-generalizable?
