# Hypothesis 01: Sanctions reallocate strategic capital unevenly across exposed and substitution-linked sectors

## Level of analysis

Primarily market and state level, with institutional and group spillovers.

## Domain

- geopolitical investment,
- financial allocation,
- industrial exposure,
- strategic substitution,
- policy shock transmission.

## Core claim

When a sanctions regime materially tightens against a strategic rival, repricing should be uneven rather than uniform: sectors with direct trade, financing, logistics, or regulatory exposure to the target should underperform, while substitution-linked, domestically supported, or allied sectors should relatively outperform.

## Why it matters

This is a foundational political-investment hypothesis. It translates a geopolitical action into a structured allocation consequence that can be simulated, compared, falsified, and used in scenario design.

It also helps distinguish a genuine sanctions transmission effect from a generic risk-off episode.

## Mechanism

The proposed mechanism has four stages:

1. A sanctions-related event changes the expected policy regime.
2. Firms, regulators, financiers, and allocators revise expectations about supply disruption, compliance costs, funding access, and secondary-sanctions risk.
3. Capital reallocates according to exposure, substitutability, domestic support, and implementation credibility.
4. Relative performance divergence appears before slower macro aggregates fully reflect the shift.

## Required inputs

This hypothesis depends on material from `00_inputs/`, especially:

- event classes covering sanctions escalation, export controls, financial restrictions, and enforcement intensification,
- actor classes covering states, regulators, firms, allocators, and market participants,
- indicator families covering trade exposure, credit conditions, sector returns, volatility, and FX sensitivity,
- asset classes covering sector baskets, sovereign proxies, logistics-linked assets, and strategic commodities,
- assumptions covering imperfect rationality, policy spillovers, and delayed information diffusion,
- coverage rules defining which sectors, states, and capital channels are in scope.

## Expected observable signature

If the hypothesis is supported, the simulation should show:

- stronger negative repricing in directly exposed sectors than in broad aggregates,
- relative resilience or outperformance in substitution beneficiaries,
- increased sector dispersion after the sanctions event,
- earlier reallocation than would be implied by slow macro reporting,
- stronger divergence when enforcement credibility is high.

## Null / baseline comparison

The null baseline is a general risk-off move that produces broad repricing without sanctions-specific concentration.

A stronger baseline compares the result against a pure volatility event with no modeled sanctions channels.

## Falsifiers

The hypothesis is weakened if:

- sector dispersion does not materially increase,
- directly exposed sectors do not underperform peers,
- substitution-linked sectors show no relative advantage,
- broad market direction explains nearly all variation,
- or enforcement credibility has little effect on outcomes.

## Gain dimensions in scope

Main gain and loss dimensions:

- financial returns,
- resilience of sector allocation,
- strategic optionality,
- exposure management quality.

## Distribution and externalities

This is usually not a pure positive-sum process.

Potential distributional features:

- exposed sectors and workers may lose,
- substitution beneficiaries may gain,
- allied producers may benefit unevenly,
- downstream consumers may face higher costs,
- long-run strategic independence may rise even while short-run welfare falls.

Collateral effects that should be tracked include:

- supply-chain disruption,
- inflation transmission,
- black-market adaptation,
- third-country spillovers,
- institutional compliance burden.

## Practical relevance

Relevant for:

- sector rotation design,
- sanctions stress testing,
- exposure reduction,
- identification of second-order beneficiaries,
- distinguishing headline sanctions from operationally credible sanctions.

## Caveats and boundary conditions

Sector moves may also reflect monetary conditions, valuation extremes, unrelated earnings events, or energy-market disturbances. The simulation should therefore compare sanctions scenarios against non-sanctions macro stress baselines.

This hypothesis is also regime-sensitive. Weakly enforced sanctions may produce far less differentiation than formally announced but operationally credible ones.
