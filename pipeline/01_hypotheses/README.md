# 01 Hypotheses

This stage converts structured inputs from `00_inputs/` into testable, falsifiable, and simulation-relevant claims.

The purpose of the hypothesis layer is to prevent the repository from collapsing into broad intuitions, ideology, or narrative-only interpretation. A hypothesis is a disciplined bridge between inputs and later model behavior.

In this repository, a hypothesis may concern:

- a person,
- a household,
- a group,
- an institution,
- a state,
- a market,
- or interactions across these levels.

The repository therefore treats investment broadly:

> An investment is any deliberate allocation of scarce resources toward future gain under uncertainty.

That gain may be financial, physical, cognitive, social, institutional, or spiritual, depending on the scope of the run.

## What a hypothesis must do

Each hypothesis document should define all of the following:

1. a precise claim,
2. the mechanism that is supposed to produce the effect,
3. the required inputs,
4. the observable signature to look for,
5. the baseline or null comparison,
6. plausible falsifiers,
7. practical relevance for allocation, prioritization, or strategy,
8. boundary conditions and caveats.

A hypothesis is not ready merely because it sounds plausible. It is ready only when the repository can answer these questions:

- What activates the claim?
- Through which channels should it operate?
- What pattern would count as support?
- What pattern would count against it?
- What alternative explanation should it be compared against?

## Working rules

### 1. Hypotheses must remain falsifiable

A claim such as "education is good" or "health matters" is too vague. A valid hypothesis must specify *how*, *for whom*, *under what conditions*, and *relative to what alternative*.

### 2. Hypotheses must identify the level of analysis

Every hypothesis should clearly state whether it operates primarily at the level of:

- individual person,
- household,
- group,
- institution,
- state,
- market,
- or cross-level interaction.

### 3. Hypotheses must not assume that all gains are commensurable

Some runs may compare financial outcomes; others may compare health, optionality, resilience, trust, or coordination quality. Hypotheses should identify which gain dimensions are in scope.

### 4. Hypotheses must distinguish gain from redistribution

A group-level gain is not automatically a net gain if it is achieved by concentrated harm elsewhere. When relevant, a hypothesis should explicitly note whether the expected outcome is:

- positive-sum,
- zero-sum,
- mixed,
- or dependent on distribution.

### 5. Hypotheses must preserve practical relevance

This repository is theoretical, but intended for practical use. Each hypothesis should therefore point toward a downstream consequence such as:

- portfolio construction,
- life planning,
- capability building,
- risk avoidance,
- coordination design,
- or institutional priority-setting.

## Folder contents

The initial hypothesis set is intentionally small. It is designed to pressure-test the architecture before the library expands.

- `hypothesis-template.md`
- `hypothesis-01-sanctions-sector-rotation.md`
- `hypothesis-02-election-uncertainty-policy-sensitive-volatility.md`
- `hypothesis-03-industrial-policy-supply-chain-repricing.md`

The current examples remain macro-political and market-facing because they inherit the original repository emphasis. They should be treated as *seed hypotheses*, not as the final boundary of the project.

## Recommended expansion path

As the repository matures, the hypothesis layer should expand in at least five directions:

### Personal investment hypotheses

Examples:

- preventive health investment increases long-run optionality more reliably than late crisis intervention,
- deep skill investment compounds more strongly than shallow multitopic exposure,
- sleep restoration improves downstream decision quality and therefore improves multiple portfolio dimensions.

### Household hypotheses

Examples:

- financial buffer size reduces forced liquidation risk,
- shared routines increase household resilience under external shocks,
- coordinated education and health planning outperforms isolated optimization.

### Group and institutional hypotheses

Examples:

- trust and coordination quality raise collective returns beyond the sum of isolated action,
- low-trust environments destroy investment efficiency through friction and defensive behavior,
- institutions with stable review loops compound knowledge faster.

### Moral and collateral-damage hypotheses

Examples:

- short-term gains achieved through high collateral damage reduce long-run stability,
- exploitative strategies may outperform temporarily but degrade system health,
- some apparently efficient allocations destroy legitimacy and therefore future coordination.

### Cross-level hypotheses

Examples:

- state policy changes alter personal and household investment priorities,
- household fragility amplifies macro stress transmission,
- educational and health deficits compound into lower group coordination capacity.

## Output expectation

A good hypothesis file should be readable by:

- a researcher,
- a model builder,
- an evaluator,
- and a practical decision-maker.

It should be precise enough to simulate, but plain enough to discuss without technical ritual.
