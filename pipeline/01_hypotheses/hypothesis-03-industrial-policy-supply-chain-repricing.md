# Hypothesis 03: Industrial-policy shifts reprice supply-chain-adjacent assets before macro data fully reflects the move

## Level of analysis

Primarily state, firm, and market level, with cross-level spillovers into labor and household outcomes.

## Domain

- industrial policy,
- supply-chain exposure,
- state capacity,
- anticipatory repricing,
- implementation credibility.

## Core claim

A credible industrial-policy shift should trigger repricing first in supply-chain-adjacent assets, strategic-input exposures, and enabling infrastructure before aggregate macro data visibly confirms the move.

## Why it matters

This hypothesis captures a lead-lag dynamic that is central to practical investment interpretation. Markets, firms, and allocators often move on expectations about state support, procurement direction, localization incentives, or export controls before production, trade, and investment aggregates catch up.

It also helps separate announcement theater from implementation-relevant change.

## Mechanism

The mechanism is:

1. A state announces or credibly signals industrial-policy support.
2. Suppliers, manufacturers, financiers, and allocators update expected demand, margins, constraints, and strategic priority.
3. Repricing emerges first in upstream suppliers, input materials, logistics nodes, utilities, and enabling infrastructure.
4. Aggregate macro confirmation arrives later because contracts, capacity buildout, financing, and reporting cycles lag.
5. The magnitude of the effect depends on policy credibility, fiscal capacity, and implementation competence.

## Required inputs

This hypothesis requires:

- event classes covering industrial-policy announcements, subsidy programs, export controls, localization mandates, and procurement redirection,
- actor classes covering states, regulators, strategic firms, suppliers, financiers, and allocators,
- indicator families covering capex expectations, order-book proxies, spreads, trade-flow indicators, and relative returns,
- asset classes covering strategic-input commodities, industrial suppliers, semiconductor-chain proxies, logistics assets, and infrastructure-linked exposures,
- assumptions covering delayed macro visibility, anticipatory markets, and heterogeneous policy credibility,
- coverage rules that define which supply chains and jurisdictions are in scope.

## Expected observable signature

The simulation should show:

- early repricing in supply-chain-adjacent and enabling assets,
- stronger reactions in strategically linked assets than in unrelated cyclicals,
- a visible lag between market repricing and aggregate macro confirmation,
- sensitivity to implementation credibility and execution capacity,
- differentiation between announcement effects and execution effects.

## Null / baseline comparison

The null baseline assumes that asset repricing and macro aggregates move together without a meaningful lead from policy-linked supply-chain assets.

A second baseline treats the announcement as narrative-only noise with no durable implementation path.

## Falsifiers

The hypothesis is weakened if:

- no lead-lag structure appears,
- repricing is broad and unspecific,
- strategically linked assets do not react more strongly than controls,
- policy credibility has little effect on timing or magnitude,
- or aggregate macro data leads instead of lags.

## Gain dimensions in scope

Main dimensions:

- anticipatory return advantage,
- strategic exposure timing,
- resilience of supply positioning,
- implementation-sensitive optionality.

## Distribution and externalities

This is often a mixed distributional process.

Possible winners:

- upstream suppliers,
- subsidized strategic industries,
- regions receiving industrial buildout,
- firms positioned inside favored supply chains.

Possible losers:

- foreign competitors,
- downstream buyers facing cost pressure,
- taxpayers funding weak implementation,
- workers and regions tied to displaced legacy chains.

Externalities may include dependency reduction, capacity redundancy, inflation pressure, environmental burden, or geopolitical fragmentation.

## Practical relevance

Useful for:

- identifying anticipatory policy beneficiaries,
- separating headlines from executable industrial-policy signals,
- stress-testing supply-chain dependence,
- timing exposure before slow macro confirmation,
- evaluating whether a policy shift creates durable or merely temporary advantage.

## Caveats and boundary conditions

Many industrial-policy narratives fail in execution. The model should explicitly separate:

- announcement credibility,
- implementation capacity,
- financing durability,
- and external dependency constraints.

The effect may also be muted when supply chains are already fully priced, when state capacity is weak, or when the policy lacks procurement and financing follow-through.
