# Hypothesis 02: Election uncertainty increases volatility more in policy-sensitive sectors than in broad aggregates

## Level of analysis

Primarily market and institutional level, with state-level information shocks.

## Domain

- electoral uncertainty,
- policy-sensitive allocation,
- narrative competition,
- volatility transmission,
- political-investment interpretation.

## Core claim

When election uncertainty rises around a credible future policy shift, volatility and repricing should increase more in policy-sensitive sectors than in broad market aggregates.

## Why it matters

This hypothesis tests whether election periods are merely general sentiment shocks or whether they operate through identifiable channels such as regulation, taxation, procurement, subsidies, tariffs, or enforcement changes.

That distinction matters for:

- whether broad hedges are sufficient,
- whether sector-specific scenario books are required,
- and whether political interpretation has real allocation value.

## Mechanism

The mechanism is:

1. An election raises uncertainty about the future policy regime.
2. Investors and institutions do not reprice the whole market evenly; they focus first on sectors whose future cash flows are policy-dependent.
3. Disagreement among actors increases because the same political information supports competing narratives.
4. This disagreement produces higher realized and implied volatility in policy-sensitive sectors relative to broad index behavior.
5. Volatility should narrow after policy clarification, even if macro conditions remain unchanged.

## Required inputs

This hypothesis depends on:

- event classes covering elections, debates, coalition uncertainty, manifesto credibility, and post-election policy clarification,
- actor classes covering parties, regulators, firms, allocators, media, and institutional interpreters,
- indicator families covering implied volatility, realized volatility, policy uncertainty proxies, and sector-relative returns,
- asset classes covering policy-sensitive sector baskets and broad market benchmarks,
- snapshot conventions that capture pre-election, campaign, immediate post-election, and clarification windows,
- assumptions covering adaptive interpretation, partial information, and heterogeneous policy credibility.

## Expected observable signature

Supportive output would include:

- volatility rising more in policy-sensitive sectors than in the broad index,
- stronger dispersion around debates, polling shocks, coalition signals, or manifesto credibility shifts,
- repricing concentrated around policy-relevant information events,
- declining sector-specific uncertainty after policy clarification,
- larger effects where expected policy discontinuity is stronger.

## Null / baseline comparison

The null baseline is a broad sentiment shock in which volatility rises proportionally across the market without systematic concentration in policy-sensitive sectors.

A second baseline is noisy headline-driven volatility with no durable relationship to policy exposure.

## Falsifiers

The hypothesis is weakened if:

- broad index volatility explains nearly all sector behavior,
- policy-sensitive sectors do not show persistent excess volatility,
- repricing timing does not align with political information updates,
- or policy clarification fails to narrow sector-specific uncertainty.

## Gain dimensions in scope

Main dimensions:

- financial return dispersion,
- hedge quality,
- information advantage,
- scenario differentiation quality.

## Distribution and externalities

This hypothesis mostly concerns distribution of repricing rather than creation of net welfare gains.

Potentially affected groups include:

- firms dependent on subsidy or procurement regimes,
- workers in policy-sensitive sectors,
- households exposed through pensions or employment,
- institutions whose mandates depend on election outcomes.

External effects may include reduced business planning confidence, delayed capex, and narrative polarization.

## Practical relevance

Useful for:

- event-driven hedging,
- sector volatility budgeting,
- election scenario construction,
- distinguishing policy sensitivity from generic noise,
- linking political interpretation to practical portfolio design.

## Caveats and boundary conditions

Election periods often overlap with macro releases, central-bank communication, war risk, or external shocks. Simulations should therefore separate election information shocks from concurrent catalysts wherever possible.

The hypothesis is strongest where elections plausibly change actual policy, not where electoral turnover is mostly symbolic.
