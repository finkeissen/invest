# Base Assumptions

## Purpose

This file records the baseline assumptions that shape interpretation before formal hypotheses or runtime logic are introduced.

## A. Methodological assumptions

1. **Strategic behavior is adaptive, not perfectly rational.**  
   Persons, groups, firms, and states respond to incentives, constraints, signaling, and institutional pressure under incomplete information.

2. **Politics, institutions, and markets interact bidirectionally.**  
   Political decisions move markets, and market outcomes feed back into political and institutional room for maneuver.

3. **Narratives matter, but they are not sufficient evidence.**  
   Story coherence may help frame a scenario, but claims still require indicators, validation anchors, or comparison against historical analogues.

4. **Coordination quality is a first-class variable.**  
   Families, firms, alliances, and institutions do not act as perfectly unified blocks. Cohesion and execution quality vary.

5. **Scope should remain deliberately incomplete at first.**  
   A compact, well-documented world is preferable to a large model built on hidden assumptions.

## B. Practical investment assumptions

6. **Investment is broader than financial allocation.**  
   A person may invest money, time, attention, discipline, care, trust, education, or sacrifice in order to gain future benefit.

7. **Not every gain is monetary.**  
   Gain may be material, health-related, educational, social, moral, institutional, or spiritual.

8. **Catastrophic loss avoidance has priority over optimization.**  
   Survival, safety, and irreversible damage control usually outrank marginal upside.

9. **Optionality has independent value.**  
   An action that preserves future room for action may be better than one that maximizes a short-term score.

10. **Life stage changes priorities.**  
    Good investment choices differ across youth, parenthood, illness, recovery, old age, and institutional responsibility.

11. **Groups are evaluated by persons, but not only by sums.**  
    Total group gain matters, but distribution, coercion, and collateral damage also matter.

12. **Collateral damage must be made visible.**  
    A strategy that benefits one actor while imposing severe hidden costs on others cannot be treated as clean gain.

13. **Not all goods are freely exchangeable.**  
    Health, trust, dignity, and meaning should not automatically be traded away for short-term efficiency.

## Initial exclusions

The first iteration does not attempt to provide:
- a complete moral theory
- a universal utility function for all persons and groups
- perfect prediction
- fully automated policy prescription
- one-number ranking across all human goods

## Working consequence

All downstream artifacts should preserve the difference between:
- gain
- cost
- risk
- time horizon
- reversibility
- collateral damage
- distribution of outcomes
