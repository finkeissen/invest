# Priority Rules for Persons

## Purpose

This file defines default sequencing logic for person-level investment.

The point is not to eliminate judgment. The point is to keep priority assumptions explicit so later models and plans do not smuggle them in invisibly.

---

## Core rule

At person level, priority should usually follow this order:
1. prevent collapse,
2. secure minimum viability,
3. remove strong headwinds,
4. build compounding capacities,
5. expand optionality,
6. pursue higher-order aims.

This sequence may be overridden, but only explicitly.

---

## Person-level ladder

### Tier 1: Catastrophe prevention

Typical first-priority items:
- acute health threats
- unsafe living conditions
- severe addiction
- unmanageable debt spiral
- legal or violent exposure
- total schedule chaos that destroys function

### Tier 2: Minimum viability

Typical minimum-viability items:
- sleep and recovery
- enough food and bodily stability
- baseline mental stability
- housing continuity
- minimal cash buffer or income continuity
- basic social support

### Tier 3: Headwind reduction

Typical headwinds to reduce early:
- chronic distraction
- unnecessary obligations
- destructive relationships
- disordered routines
- poor information diet
- unresolved administrative burdens
- health neglect with predictable downstream cost

### Tier 4: Compounding capacities

Typical compounding investments:
- education
- professional skill
- habit formation
- language ability
- savings discipline
- reputation for reliability
- deeper health maintenance

### Tier 5: Optionality expansion

Typical optionality investments:
- liquidity
- portable skills
- mobility access
- broad networks
- multiple income paths
- institutional credibility

### Tier 6: Mission and long-range projects

Examples:
- family building
- institution building
- public service
- artistic or scientific projects
- major vocational commitments
- spiritual discipline or religious vocation

---

## Default heuristics

- Do not sacrifice health too cheaply for short-run gain.
- Do not call self-destruction discipline.
- Maintenance counts as investment.
- A smaller reversible step is often better than a large irreversible gamble under uncertainty.
- When two actions have similar upside, prefer the one with lower hidden downside.
- When severely destabilized, simplify before optimizing.

---

## Review questions

For each major person-level investment, ask:
- What prerequisite does this assume?
- What headwind does this remove?
- What future capacity does this build?
- What downside does it create?
- What becomes easier if this works?
- What becomes worse if it fails?
