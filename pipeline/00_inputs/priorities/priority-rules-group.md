# Priority Rules for Groups

## Purpose

This file defines default sequencing logic for group-level and collective investment.

A group should not be optimized as if it were a single giant person. Group gain must remain connected to the condition of actual members and affected outsiders.

---

## Core rule

At group level, priority should usually follow this order:
1. prevent group breakdown and severe member harm,
2. secure minimum coordination viability,
3. reduce concentrated harms and hidden burdens,
4. build durable shared capacities,
5. improve distribution and legitimacy,
6. pursue strategic expansion.

---

## Group-level ladder

### Tier 1: Prevent breakdown

Examples:
- loss of basic trust
- violence or coercive capture
- total leadership illegibility
- financial collapse of the collective
- member burnout at critical nodes
- severe internal conflict with no repair process

### Tier 2: Minimum coordination viability

Examples:
- role clarity
- reliable communication
- conflict handling
- minimum shared norms
- basic resource continuity
- identifiable decision rights

### Tier 3: Burden and damage reduction

Examples:
- concentrated sacrifice of a few members
- invisible care burden
- uncompensated maintenance work
- externalized harm to neighbors or weak stakeholders
- reputational decay from hypocrisy or opacity

### Tier 4: Shared capacity building

Examples:
- education systems
- succession planning
- trained leadership
- durable processes
- emergency reserves
- trust-building routines
- knowledge continuity

### Tier 5: Distribution and legitimacy

Examples:
- fairer burden sharing
- transparent decision logic
- repair pathways for harmed members
- voice mechanisms
- accountability structures

### Tier 6: Strategic expansion

Examples:
- growth of institutional scope
- wider influence
- capital formation
- new ventures
- broader coalition building

---

## Default heuristics

- Do not call a strategy successful if it depends on invisible sacrifice by a minority.
- Protect maintenance workers and caregivers from systematic extraction.
- A fragile high-performance group is often worse than a slower but durable group.
- Legitimacy is productive capacity, not just decoration.
- Group growth that destroys member stability is often fake growth.

---

## Review questions

For each major group-level investment, ask:
- Which persons benefit?
- Which persons bear the cost?
- Is the burden concentrated?
- Does the investment improve coordination without degrading dignity?
- Does the investment create positive-sum value or merely transfer it?
- What repair obligation follows if harm is imposed?
